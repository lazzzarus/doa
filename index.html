Banknotes of

GERMAN EAST AFRICA

A REFERENCE SITE

As a collector of German East Africa banknotes for the past few years I have found being a non-German speaker made it considerably difficult for me to gain a more indepth knowledge of the nuances of the subject. This website is set up mainly to aid English speakers with the intricacies of this area of collecting. I am aware it is not the most popular country to collect however I have found the different aspect of this area facinating as they include banknotes with a colonial past, as well as banknotes produced across the span of a war as well as...Beautifully printed notes as well as notes produced in the theater of war. While it covers area such a traditional collecting such as specimen notes. It is my pleasure to be able to share what I have learnt along the way as always on the shoulders of others. I know there are many experts in this field out there and if you do happen to stumble upon my website and see areas of improvement or additional information that you would like to contribute (in English or German!) it would be great to be able to further enhance the experince of any visitor. There is no commerical intent in my website, being a member of the IBNS this website has been set up purely as a reference. Please browse, enjoy, learn, repeat! Please note that the images and content of this site are not in the public domain and may not be copied.

  1. German East African Company

    The Society for German Colonization was founded on 28 March 1884 in Berlin by Carl Peters (a German explorer, politician and author). This led to the formation of the German East Africa Company (Deutsch-Ostafrikanische Gesellschaft - DOAG) As seen, the flag of the German East Africa Company featured a stylised representation of the constellation of the Southern Cross.

    Flags description

  2. German East Africa

    On 3 March 1885, the German government announced that it had granted an imperial charter, which was signed by Chancellor Otto von Bismarck on 27 February 1885. The charter was granted to Peters' company and was intended to establish a protectorate in the African Great Lakes region. Peters then recruited specialists who began exploring south to the Rufiji River and north to Witu, near Lamu on the coast. In 1890, London and Berlin concluded the Heligoland-Zanzibar Treaty, which returned Heligoland to Germany and decided the border between GEA and the East Africa Protectorate controlled by Britain, although the exact boundaries remained unsurveyed until 1910. the establishment of German East Africa, a territory which eventually comprised the areas of modern Tanzania, Burundi, and Rwanda.
